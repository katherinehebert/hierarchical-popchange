## Abstract (in progress)

Biodiversity change is unfolding as an interrelated combination of gains and losses in the abundances of species, which is reorganizing the composition and structure of diversity. This reorganization is most profound when species’ trends are highly variable within the community. Summarising these changes in a meaningful way represents a major challenge, which becomes even more difficult when we need to understand the potential impacts of these changes on ecosystem functioning. When biodiversity change metrics summarize species’ trends into an average, they cannot capture the hierarchy of variability within and between species, and therefore cannot indicate changes in the structure of biodiversity. We propose an approach to summarize the overall trajectory and variability in population trends by leveraging residual variation to better capture unobserved drivers of changes. We describe a hierarchical population trend model to estimate the change in abundance for an ensemble of populations over time as their average trend, their co-variations, and the variance of species’ trends within the assemblage. We demonstrate the model on a case study of 29 groundfish species on the Newfoundland shelf between 1981 and 2013, which is a period characterized by a profound restructuring of the community in response to the near collapse of several species in the 1980s and 1990s. Importantly, we include a latent factor approach We propose that this approach could be used to capture multiple levels of variability in population-level changes to improve our ability to estimate biodiversity change and interpret the collective implications of these changes for ecosystem stability.
