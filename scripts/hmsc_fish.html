Hierarchical linear model on fish population trends

Model we are attempting to build:

\[N_{i,t} = f(t, \alpha_i) + g(\lambda_i, \beta_i) + \varepsilon_i\]

Where:

  • \(N\) : population size estimate

  • \(i\) : population

  • \(t\) : time step

  • \(f(t, \alpha_i)\) : function estimating the temporal trend in population size

    • \(\alpha\): estimated parameter describing each population’s temporal slope

  • \(g(\lambda, \beta_i)\) : function estimating latent variation in population size

    • \(\lambda\) : latent variable (to capture covariation between populations)
    • \(\beta\) : estimated parameter describing each population’s latent variation

  • \(\varepsilon\): residual variation

Because the model also estimates latent variation, we are accounting for the covariation between populations when estimating \(N\).

This model then contains key information to calculate and extend an index like the Living Planet Index, to show the average change in population size from a baseline value of 1 in the start year.

The first part of the model estimates the temporal trend of each population’s size (\(\alpha_i\)), which contains information about population-level change and community-level change:

\[\alpha_i \sim N(\mu_{\alpha}, \sigma^2_{\alpha}) \]

where: - {} is the average population trend - ^2{} is the variance in this average trend, giving us a measure of how variable the population trends are within the sample.

To calculate the index of change from a baseline of 1 (as in the LPI), the average population trend can then be plugged in to the LPI calculation as follows:

\[ I_t = {\mu_{\alpha}}*t + 1 \]

This trend can then be visualized and communicated with its known (\(\sigma^2_{\alpha}\)).

# get number of populations & length of time series
npops <- ncol(Year_Geom_Means_all)
tsl <- nrow(Year_Geom_Means_all)

Building the model with HMSC

# format data
XData <- as.matrix(time)
YData <- as.matrix(Year_Geom_Means_all) %>% apply(2, scale, center = FALSE)
randomEff <- as.factor(1:tsl) # assign a location to pairings of pops

# prepare data
formDat <- as.HMSCdata(X = XData, Y = YData, Random = randomEff, 
                       interceptX = TRUE)

# run model
m <- hmsc(formDat,
                family = "gaussian",
                niter = 15000,
                nburn = 5000,
                thin = 5,
                verbose = FALSE)
# save model
saveRDS(m, here("models/hmsc.RDS"))

These two trends show different things: the LPI shows the overall trend in population growth rates, while the HMSC prediction shows the overall trend in population abundance.

  • Abundances are perhaps more intuitive to interpret - working with only the abundances removes the confusion of what the LPI represents (it’s not abundances - so what is it? e.g. Puurtinen et al. 2021), and avoids the imbalanced weight of declines and growths that biases the geometrically averaged growth rate in the LPI.

  • The trend itself is pretty similar between the two methods - drastic decline in the 1980s to mid-1990s, followed by a lot of variation in the years that follow the big crash as populations readjust to the new community composition and structure.

The most important difference lies in the interpretation of the uncertainty intervals in each trend.

  • In the LPI, the interval represents the 95% confidence interval around the mean trend, showing the variation among population trends that are included in the mean. In other words, this interval shows the spread of trends around the mean trend. Populations declined at similar steep rates in the 80s-90s, then follow a broader variety of trends in the years following the crash. However, this interval does not include any of the error from the data itself, as it does not retain any information about residual error from the modelling process. It’s a representation of how variable the sample is, rather than how certain the trend is based on the data.

  • The HMSC trend shows a credible interval around the median trend for the sample of populations, which represents the uncertainty surrounding the trend due to variability in the data and the correlations between population abundance trends, which were captured by the latent trend we introduced into the model. This means the credible interval is showing us the statistical uncertainty surrounding the trend, rather than a metric of how variable the trends are between populations.

  • Importantly, the HMSC interval is much wider than the LPI’s confidence intervals, because of this difference in what it represents. This wider interval gives a much different impression of how certain we are in the population abundance trend - it is clear that certainty is variable through time, particularly at the beginning and end of the time series.

The HMSC trend allows us to report quite a bit:

  • On average, population abundance declined during this time period (slope = -0.072).
  • Populations’ temporal trends were pretty variable (variance of the slope = 0.116). The 95% credible interval around the slope was: -0.504, 0.558).

Most populations’ abundance fluctuations covaried positively (shown in blue, meaning their trends fluctuated similarly), though a few species’ trends fluctuated separately from the other populations (in red, meaning they were negatively covarying):

For example, Cottunculus microps (polar sculpin, a deepwater fish), Cyclopterus lumpus (lumpfish), Eumicrotremus sp. (lumpfishes), and Notacanthidae (deep sea spiny eels) all showed abundance trends that covaried negatively with the other populations in the ecosystem during this time period.

To summarise, the hierarchical approach allows us to estimate:

  • The overall trend of population abundance for all populations simultaneously (LPI-pop), with a credible interval
  • The abundance trend for each population, allowing us to see which were declining, stable, or growing, with a credible interval for each
  • The variance in this overall trend, which tells us about how population change is unfolding at the community level (LPI-comm)
  • Correlations between populations’ abundance trends (via latent variables)
